Understanding DDM Variables

D₀
Current Annual Dividend
↑ Higher D₀ → ↑ Higher Price

D₀ is the anchor of the entire model — it's the most recent dividend paid per share. The relationship is strictly linear: doubling D₀ exactly doubles the estimated fair value. In practice, use the trailing twelve-month (TTM) dividend. For the S&P 500, D₀ reached $74.83 in 2024, up from $0.69 in 1926.

g
Dividend Growth Rate
↑ Higher g → ↑↑ Price (nonlinear)

g is the most powerful — and dangerous — input. As g approaches r from below, the denominator (r − g) shrinks toward zero and the price explodes toward infinity. A 1% change in g near r can swing fair value by hundreds of dollars. S&P 500 dividend growth has historically averaged ~5–6% per year. Never set g ≥ r.

r
Required Rate of Return
↑ Higher r → ↓ Lower Price

r is your discount rate — the minimum return you demand to own the stock. It is typically the risk-free rate (10-year Treasury yield) plus an equity risk premium of ~4–5%. When the Fed raises interest rates, r rises and stock valuations fall — exactly what happened in 2022–2023 when the S&P 500 dropped ~19%.
